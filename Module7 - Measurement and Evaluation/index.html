Purpose and Theory

In this module, you will look at combining your recommended policy instruments into an overall system. To do this you need to look at all your recommended policy instruments together so that adjustments can be made to address:

  • Coherence: similar and related issues are handled in similar ways
  • Economy: a minimum number of compliance mechanisms has been created to manage costs
  • Efficiency: a minimum number of instruments will be put in place to avoid confusing impacted parties
  • Alignment: a balance of legally binding and non-legally binding instruments to make sure you will gain political support

Identify System Options

A combination of mutually supportive policy instruments is more effective in reaching set objectives than a single policy instrument. It is best to use as few policy instruments as possible rather than too many. What strategies can we use for mixing and matching various policy instruments to create a series of system options for a recommendation?

To identify policy options, consider project timelines, government priorities and management, and start with the baseline (e.g., option 1). To option 1, add one or more realistically achievable policy instruments to make up option 2. Gradually continue to add more complex policy instruments to generate further options.

Tip! What to do when deciding what policy instruments to include or exclude?

When deciding what policy instruments to include or exclude:

  • Identify essential instruments (e.g., law that requires a licence to conduct controlled activity)
  • Supporting instruments (e.g., licensing program).

During this process, identified options may be different from one another in scope and complexity (e.g., implementation of a new law versus an education and training program). Some trade-offs will need to be made based on available resources or political appetite or support. Options may even include mutually exclusive policy instruments.

Engage

Plan to have regular engagement with decision-makers to communicate progress and expectations. It will be important for decision-makers to understand the proposed solutions, the impacts, and the stakeholders affected. This will make sure that the proposed solutions are well understood and the value that they bring is clear.

Engagement with the appropriate stakeholders during every point of program development, including during policy options development, is critical to success. Conduct a preliminary scan of how stakeholders are likely to react and be affected by the proposed options. Test identified solutions through their points of view. During the scan, consider the following questions:

  • How practical and achievable are the options (e.g., costs to achieve compliance)?
  • What actions do stakeholders need to take to become part of the program (e.g., application for a licence, mandatory training, and changes to infrastructure)?
  • Is there anything that may prevent or make it difficult for stakeholders to implement the options (e.g., lack of support from management for training, insufficient funds to update a facility)?

Tip! What approach should I take?

Engage with stakeholders to explain and gather input on the proposed options. The participatory approach will contribute to buy-in to the future program. It will also allow challenges with implementation to be identified and addressed early in the process.

Stakeholders should be viewed as the users of the proposed program. For example, if you are developing an option that includes significant changes to the established program, you will need to understand the consequences of the changes for those affected. Further details on how to engage stakeholders can be found in Module 2 - Issue Identification, Risk Assessment and Prioritization.

Develop an Implementation Plan

Develop a plan for implementing the program for each option developed. Start by developing a full plan for the preferred option, and then consider how the components of the plan would be different for the remaining options.

The implementation plan should include:

Click the arrows to learn more.

Scope of the program that specifies what it includes, what it addresses, and what it excludes.

A schedule with the duration and sequence of activities (e.g., timeline for development and approval of laws if selected). Consider that different policy instruments may take different periods to develop (e.g. training material can be developed quickly while legislation may take years).

Approvals and early support from decision-makers.

Resources required to implement and sustain the program (e.g., people, equipment, money).

Milestones that mark major events or points in time, to monitor the project’s progress.

Deliverables, including products (e.g., training manuals, law).

Allies and partners (e.g., agencies, departments, other government authorities) who are linked with the work.

There are several advantages to developing an implementation plan for each option. For example, if the recommended program option is not accepted, you are in a good position to have an informed discussion about other options. An implementation plan also encourages another comparison of the options to improve and refine them further.

Identify and Recommend the Preferred Options

When several options for a biosafety and biosecurity system have been developed, identify the best option and a few other strong options to present to senior decision-makers for their consent.

To formulate identified options, keep in mind your target audience, questions that they may have, and include:

  • A description of the option
  • A listing of proposed policy instruments with a brief description of how each instrument will support program objectives
  • The pros and cons of each option, for example:
    • Cost impact on government and stakeholders
    • Implementation considerations (e.g., ease, sustainability), and implementation timelines
    • Legal impacts
    • Anticipated stakeholder support for and opposition to each option
    • Outstanding biosafety and biosecurity risks that the option does not address

Key Stakeholders

Because of the need to make sure that this task is conducted consistently with the political and financial support that a new biosafety and biosecurity framework can expect, it is recommended that the task be carried out by the Working Group (hence Workshop Materials are not provided). Any problems, issues, gaps with tasks and what the government is capable of addressing should be clearly presented to stakeholders. Once draft options have been developed however, these can be presented for validation that the options are realistic, before being presented to decision-makers.

EXERCISES – Exercise 5.1 Developing Options

Once you have established your baseline framework, it can be useful to develop some options based on different points of emphasis. This can be a good preparation for discussions with senior decision-makers when seeking support for implementation.

Consider the following and create options as necessary. If you cannot develop an option that comes close to meeting the requirements under one of the constraints make sure you record this and are ready to provide your rationale.

  • If you had to meet the objectives with fewer resources, how would the framework change (including changes to target timelines)?
  • If there was a reduced appetite for new mandatory/legally binding instruments, how would the framework change?
  • What other limitations should you consider?

Tip! What else should I consider?

As you proceed make sure you consider what are key program elements (e.g., law that requires a licence to conduct controlled activity) and those that need to be included as supporting policy instruments (e.g., a licensing program). During this process, identified options may be different from one another in scope and complexity (e.g., implementation of a new law versus an education and training program). Some trade-offs will need to be made.

Once you have established your baseline framework and, if necessary, some options, you are now ready to proceed with Module 6: Implementation Approach.

Summary

This module introduced you to how to combine policy instruments into national system options.

  • To combine your recommended policy instruments into an overall system you need to look at all your recommended policy instruments together so that adjustments can be made to address coherence, economy, efficiency, and alignment.
  • A combination of mutually supportive policy instruments is more effective in reaching set objectives than a single policy instrument – it is best to use as few as possible policy instruments rather than too many.
  • While identifying options, consider how the proposed options rank against criteria, address risk, relate to exiting policies and programs, can be implemented, and use existing resources.
  • Have regular engagement with decision-makers to communicate progress and expectations so they understand the proposed solutions, the impacts, and the stakeholders affected.
  • Engagement with the appropriate stakeholders during every point of program development, including during policy options development, is critical to success.
  • An implementation plan should include scope, schedule, approvals, resources, milestones, deliverables, allies and partners.
  • To formulate identified options, know the details of each option, why the selected policy instruments are appropriate, and the pros and cons of each (e.g., cost, sustainability, legal impacts, level of support, and risks).
  • The task of combining policy instruments into national system options should be carried out by the Working Group because of the need to make sure that this task is conducted consistently with the political and financial support that a new biosafety and biosecurity framework can expect.
