Scope and Objectives for New System Components

Table of Contents

Introduction

In developing a national biosafety and biosecurity system and selecting policy instruments, it is important to set achievable goals. Take into consideration those who administer and enforce the policy instrument as well as those it impacts. Those who will be subject to the new policy instrument must be able to achieve and maintain compliance. To do so, there must be political support and resources for compliance mechanisms (e.g., inspectors) and promotion (e.g., education). Once you have identified the key leverage points (Module 2: Issue Identification, Risk Assessment, and Prioritization), you must decide how much of the risk (and in which sectors) you want to address in your national system, as well as timing.

This will consider:

  • The most significant and urgent risks
  • The resources you have available
  • Any target dates that you have for implementation
  • Any resistance that you may encounter

Learning Objectives

The purpose of this module is to set the scope of your national biosafety and biosecurity system. You will determine what issues to address and set clear objectives to guide your work. After completing Module 3: Scope And Objectives For New System Components, you will understand how to identify the scope and objectives for new system components including:

  • Prioritizing risks
  • Setting SMART objectives and sub-objectives
  • Integrating the perspectives of subject matter experts
  • Integrating key considerations for setting specific objectives, mitigating risk, establishing scope, and proceeding with consultations

Objectives should be:

An objective describes the scope of what will be achieved by finding the cause of sector-specific issues. Depending on your results so far, you may set objectives:

  • For an issue you identified in terms of your overall biosafety and biosecurity system
  • For a root cause of one or more issues that has been identified as a key leverage point (e.g., awareness, education)
  • For a contributing cause that was identified as part of the root cause analysis (e.g., it may not prevent an issue, but could decrease its effects in a cost-effective way)

An objective precisely defines what you intend to do. Consider the difference between these two statements:

  • Statement #1 – “Standardize biosafety training across all sectors in the next five years”
  • Statement #2 – “Standardize biosafety training for all government employees in the next five years”

Statement #2 defines the individuals impacted by the training by including “training for all government employees” rather than “training across all sectors”.

Objectives should be “SMART” (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and with a Target Time) but an objective does not detail “how” to solve a problem. If you experience challenges when setting objectives, it may mean that defining the problems or risks need more work.

Sub-Objectives

Once you have set main objectives, you may realise that sub-objectives are required to meet them. It can be useful to set sub-objectives for each objective to keep the project on track. For example, an overall objective might be: “Over the next three years, establish a culture of biosafety in academia as measured by: appointment of trained Biosafety Officers, commitment to training facility staff, and regular reporting of incidents and laboratory acquired infections.” Some sub-objectives might be:

  • Over the next year educate the senior executives of universities about the importance and long-term cost effectiveness of biosafety with the aim of obtaining commitment to change;
  • Over the next 18 months identify the training requirements needed to meet the overall objective in the academic sector and identify existing training if available;
  • If training is not available, over the following 2 years, develop an on-line biosafety training program; and
  • Over the final year, promote the program to those at universities who work with pathogens and toxins and those who manage them with the aim of X% enrolment.

In addition, the objectives that are set for the development of a system can form the basis for evaluating the success of the system once put in place.

Setting objectives is also an important part of communicating with decision makers, stakeholders, those who will be regulated, and those who will be tasked with putting the system in place. Clear objectives help manage expectations and calm fears that the burden will be too great.

Key Stakeholders

The key stakeholders for this module are subject matter experts for the sectors that you expect to be impacted by the scope of oversight that you will set. Their perspectives are important because any oversight instruments that you put in place must:

  • Be practical and realistic
  • Be achievable by the target sectors and sub-sectors (e.g., they must have the knowledge and resources to be able to become and remain compliant)

Finally, those familiar with the current political climate will be able to advise you on expectations and commitment. It is important that you are aware of and work within the scope of what senior decision-makers will be willing to consider. This will impact the overall sustainability of this work.

Exercise 3.1 Setting Scope and Objectives - Step 1

Before working on the specific objectives, make sure you reconsider:

  • How quickly do you want to put an initial framework in place? Now you know more about the root causes and leverage points and as you begin to consider how you might address issues and risks, you will be in a better place to estimate the time it will take to implement oversight.
  • How many resources do you have to dedicate to this project (e.g., will one team have to work on all the issues or can multiple teams with subject matter experts work at the same time)? The level of resources assigned to addressing issues will also influence the time needed.
  • How much support can you expect for implementation? Will you have to work with the current infrastructure and workforce only or can you expect new resources (e.g., to create inspector training and to employ inspectors).
  • Are there other factors that might determine which issues you address first and how you assign resources such as: political and global attention, political and international commitments made by your Government; and related work that is already underway? For instance, in a number of cases during the pilot workshops to refine this tool, work related to the Biological and Toxin Weapons Convention (BTWC), United Nations Security Council Resolution 1540 (UNSCR) and the International Health Regulations Joint External Evaluation (IHR JEE) was already underway that could be leveraged.

Exercise 3.1 Setting Scope and Objectives - Step 2

Consider each key leverage point that you identified in Module 2: Issue Identification, Risk Assessment, and Prioritization; consider all the ways that you might mitigate the risks or issues if:

  • You tried to remove or reduce the issue in all sectors. Estimate how long this might take and what the resource requirements might be.
  • You tried to remove or reduce the issue in only those sectors which you have identified as priority. Estimate how long this might take and what the resource requirements might be.
  • You tried to remove or reduce the issue for the sector(s) where the challenge would be the least (e.g., government facilities where you might have more direct control). Estimate how long this might take and what the resource requirements might be.

Exercise 3.1 Setting Scope and Objectives - Step 3

Establish a scope and objective related to each priority issue and leverage point (e.g., to make standardized biosafety training mandatory for all government laboratory personnel. Training of existing personnel to be completed within 3 years).

Specific – target a specific area for improvement (e.g., make standardized biosafety training mandatory for all government laboratory employees).

Measurable –identify the indicators of progress and success (e.g., identify how many employees have completed training in 2 years).

Achievable – objective or expectation is realistic, this should be established as part of the discussions (e.g., stakeholder input supports this as being feasible).

Relevant –state what results can be realistically achieved given available resources (e.g., if this relates to a priority issue and a key leverage point the relevance has been established).

Target Time – state the time-frame within which the objective will be achieved (e.g., training of existing personnel to be completed within 3 years).

Note: Although you considered how you might address this issue the “how” is not included in the objective. This is because you may change the “hows” in Module 5: Combine Policy Instruments Into National System Options.

Exercise 3.1 Setting Scope and Objectives - Step 4/5

Step 4

At this point, it can be valuable to consult all those who will be impacted by any proposed changes. Meeting with senior decision-makers (e.g., national ministries) as you proceed will keep them informed and confirm their ongoing support. Consulting with those whose behaviour you want to change will allow you to obtain important feedback about their challenges and any impediments or resistance to compliance that was not identified by the subject matter experts and other representatives you have worked with so far.

Module 2 - Issue Identification, Risk Assessment and Prioritization provides guidance on how you can consult with those who will be impacted when developing your system to increase the probability that what you develop will be realistic and practical, and to reduce the probability of resistance to implementation.

Step 5

Refine your objectives based on Step Four. Consultations may provide you with information that requires you to revisit the initial assumptions that you based your objectives on. This will require you to refine your objectives.

Once you have established a set of SMART objectives you are ready to move on to Module 4: Policy Instruments.
